Vision Statement

Bottom-up fabrication AND system design techniques
will make solar energy structures more efficient than
traditional fossil fuel systems.

The traditional manufacturing world has not been able to use many advances of nanotechnology because of toolset limitations and because they lack the ‘nano-speak’ vernacular needed to quickly integrate new ideas. Likewise, nano-scientists do not understand the intricacies of modern macro-scale system design and usually do not deal with mass production issues and requirements limiting their ability to scale up their projects.

We want to bridge the gap between the traditional commercial engineering and new ideas coming from nanoscale innovations.

In serving as a bridge between traditional manufacturing and nano-scale assembly we can identify new approaches to not only fabrication but to system design as well.

SolarMelts believes that maximizing the efficiencies of nanoscale devices & systems is best done using
bottom-up1 approaches to fabrication & system design. We believe in working on technology that can be used as building blocks for multiple projects. This way each success opens up new fields & opportunities for future projects.

Our first products SolarSand, sElectricHeat, and sSeedBalls are the result of our understanding of chemical methods to incorporate nano-materials. We have several ideas on how to enhance our current products so contact us for more details.



1. "Nano-speak terms"
Top-down fabrication: Traditional manufacturing techniques used for size reduction from bulk materials. Examples include: lithography, high-resolution processing

Bottom-up fabrication: Assembly from individual atoms/molecules. Examples include: chemical/solution processes, nano- or bio-templates, & self-assembly methods.

Top-down design: Miniaturizing traditional systems and incorporating nanostructures in old designs.

Bottom-up design: Building new systems around designs that maximize the best properties of nano-materials to maintain their high efficiencies. Often incorporating the collective output of several small but highly efficient sub-units.
